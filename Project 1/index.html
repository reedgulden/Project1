The First Time TV Felt Like It Saw Me

Why a Make-Believe Glee Club Mattered in My Actual Teen Years



Credit : Kwaku Alston/FOX



I started watching Glee a few years after it first aired in 2009 and was hooked almost immediately. I was only about nine, but I felt an unexpected connection to its high school characters. The show was bold, funny, and often ridiculous—singing football players, mash-ups that had no right to work but somehow did—and I enjoyed every minute of it. Beneath all the glitter, it gave space to a wide range of characters and stories that were rarely seen on TV, and that left a lasting impression on me.



The storyline that struck me the hardest was that of Santana and Brittany. Their long-developing, passionate relationship showed two young women figuring themselves out, flaws and all. It was never treated as a cheap gimmick or a ratings grab. They were allowed to be imperfect, raw, and real. They argued, made mistakes, faced disapproval, and kept growing—exactly what navigating sexuality can look like for a young queer person. They weren’t written just to entertain; they were fully realized characters whose love felt authentic.


"When I'm with Brittany, I finally understand what people are talking about, when they talk about love"

-Santana Lopez

Santana and Brittany’s relationship began as a close friendship starting through their highschool cheerleading team, which gradually developed into deeper feelings.


Initially, their romance was kept secret, as Santana struggled with her sexuality and fear of judgment, while Brittany remained open and accepting. Over the whole series, Santana's journey toward self-acceptance became a central focus, and the two eventually embraced their love publicly. Their story concluded with a heartfelt wedding, marking one of the show's most enduring and meaningful relationships

>

As a girl slowly realizing I’m attracted to women, this meant everything. I’d never seen two women love each other like that on a major network show without it being tragic or treated like a lesson. Watching them thrive made my own future feel normal and possible.



Glee still gives me plenty of laughs and songs to sing along to, but Santana and Brittany give me something much deeper: proof that feminine love belongs in everyday stories, not as an exception or a fantasy, but as part of real life. That’s why Glee still matters to me today.
